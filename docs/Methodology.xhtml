Methodology

What Our Project Is

Our project is aimed at showing the ambiguity in the Iliad around the word Andros. Andros means both man and husband, but overtime different translations have come to choose one translated word over another depending on a specific relationship. We use the three versions of the Iliad (the Greek, Butler, and Wilson version) to analyze the word usage with the relationship between Achilles and Patroklos, who have been thought to have been in a romantic relationship, and the relationship between Menelaus and Helen.

Our Process

We started off gathering the translations that we wanted to use. We used the Greek TEI text, the Butler (english) TEI translation, and quotes from the newer Wilson (english) translation. Katie went into the Greek version of the text and found every instance of the word Andros. At the same time, Isa and Cheyenne went into the Butler version. searched for, and markedup every instance of the words man and husband and performed a close reading of the text to desipher the context and character the word was referring to. Kosi made the schemas for both the Butler and Greek versions of the Iliad and did the XSLT for our corpus. After this, we wanted to center our project on Achilles and Patroklos, so Cheyenne went into the physical Wilson text and found the instances of the word man and husband in relation to the Butler version for Achilles and Patroklos and Meneleus and Helen of which there were 16. She then made an XML document marking these instances up and giving each one a unique ID and adding those to the Butler TEI. Katie went and found these instances in the Greek version, gave them the same unique ID's, and created the XSLT. After our research was concluded, we started designing the website. Isa designed the contact and characters pages using XHTML and CSS, Cheyenne and Kosi created the methodology page and the chart.

Findings

Our group noticed that for Achilles and Patroklos, the word man was used as well as other phrases like comrade or even friend, but between Meneleus and Helen husband was used. Specifically in the Butler version, we notice man being used repeatedly for Achilles and Patroklos. However, in the Wilson version, the newer translation, man is also used but so is comrade and even the phrase the man that I love spoken by Achilles towards Patroklos. Based on these findings, we want to show how the term Andros is ambiguous and depends on the context in which it is used.
